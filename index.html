Our research principles

We receive and distribute research that has a basis in healing. Paradoxical results that can cause harm, no matter how novel or impactful, will not be distributed.

01

Collect

We follow literature and stories across clinical, social, ethnographic, and traditional disciplines to surface to teams what's worth a closer look.

02

Validate

We assess methodology, replication, and cultural context and through our network of practitioners collect field tested results.

03

Share

We translate validated research into resources people can use; findings without a clear path in healing do not make it past this stage.

Three traditions, one standard

Where we look for what works

Healing wisdom doesn't live in one place. We organize our research across three traditions so practitioners and researchers can find what aligns with their work and the people they serve. These two systems differ fundamentally in philosophy, diagnosis, treatment, and goals, though they can complement each other in integrative care.

Eastern traditions

Practices and frameworks rooted in the medical and contemplative traditions of Asia — including Traditional Chinese Medicine, Ayurveda, and Buddhist contemplative science. We focus on findings where mechanism and outcome are both well-characterized.

Meta-analysis Mindfulness-based interventions and chronic pain → Clinical review Acupuncture for treatment-resistant conditions → Practitioner guide Pranayama protocols in anxiety care →

Western traditions

Evidence-based medicine, clinical psychology, and applied behavioral science. We pay particular attention to integrative approaches and to research on patient-reported outcomes that conventional metrics often miss.

Longitudinal study Social connection and recovery outcomes Practitioner only

Tribal traditions

Indigenous and tribal healing practices across the Americas and beyond, shared in collaboration with the communities they belong to. We follow the lead of tribal partners on what should be public, what should be protected, and how findings are presented.

Ethnographic study Ceremonial practice and community resilience Practitioner only
Collaborative paper Plant medicine in indigenous mental health care →
Oral history Healing practices preserved by tribal elders Practitioner only
